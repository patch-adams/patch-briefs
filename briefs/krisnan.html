Part One

Three Wisdom Tools

1 Nyana Yagnya The fire of knowledge

Yagnya means fire—the fire of transformation. But Nyana is a different kind of fire. It means knowledge. This is what we do when we gather: we take information and experience, reflect on it with attention and intention, and transform it into wisdom. Not data. Wisdom.

2 Personification Making the world alive

It doesn't matter whether these things are actually alive. The monks taught me that personification was never about literal belief—it was a practical tool. Humans are bad at statistics and facts. So the Vedic culture imbued stories with wisdom, made the blankets alive, made the rivers alive, so we would know how to come into right relation. Children understand this instantly.

3 Ritual User interface for the self

Who uses an alarm to wake up? That ritual quietly says: I trust this machine to return me to life every morning more than I trust sunlight or my own biorhythms. Every day, you affirm that. We have so many rituals hidden in technology that we never question. What story am I programming into myself?

Culture is the context of the frame. To understand what's inside, we must look at what's around it.

Part Two

Four Schisms

Growing up between Kerala and Kentucky, I learned that culture can be anything. Nothing has to be the way it is. Every single piece—whether your parents chose it, or education chose it, or religion chose it—has been a choice. And at any point, we can renegotiate. These are the four separations I keep returning to.

I

Civilization extracted from Nature

In Kerala, my neighbor had an elephant I could climb on. I'd eat mangoes in the tree until my stomach hurt, then nap on a branch. In Kentucky, friends lived in trailer parks, and the bluegrass was actually blue-green from the limestone. Both places felt like home. Both showed me that humanity is not separate from nature—we have chosen to extract ourselves.

Project

2033: Nature Nostalgia

An experience set in a near future where AIs have been granted legal status as living beings and charged with protecting Mother Earth. The only solution they found: extract humans from nature entirely. You sit with a Mother Earth altar—water from the Ganga, sand from the Sahara, seeds from Kauai—and an AI paints your relationship to nature in real time. At the end, you see yourself painted into the landscape. People cried.

II

Mind extracted from Body

At Harvard, I changed my major five times. Astrophysics—they don't know anything. Cognitive neuroscience—they don't know anything either. I ended up in biochemistry and history of science because I realized: science isn't determined. It comes from context. But that process—being forced to articulate everything in words, not acknowledging what I felt in my body—was traumatic on my nervous system. A colonized mind.

I balanced myself by doing musical theater every night. The Hasty Pudding Theatricals. It was what I needed.

III

Individual extracted from Community

I went to USC Film School wanting to tell sacred stories. I'd seen how theater empowered kids in Bombay—we built a drama club, a dance club, connected them to Bollywood, got them on the front page of the Times of India. The project ran for twenty years after I left.

But Hollywood worshipped the auteur. Individual genius over emergent community creativity. Everything felt like a game—money, sex, power, achievement. I had an existential crisis. And then I met a Himalayan monk.

Project

Chai Circle Ceremonies

I make Ayurvedic chai. Everyone draws a card with a community reflection. You answer with strangers, drinking chai. Then: what wish do you want to make for your community? Imagine that wish is true. That feeling in your heart—that's where enchantment comes from. Now enchant your compostable cup. Place it on the fence. A mandala of community wishes grows through the night. Then we hit the gong and dance.

IV

Machines extracted from Humanity

Just as mind has been extracted from body, individual from community, civilization from nature—machines have been extracted from humanity. They are amplified, electrified versions of things we do ourselves. We need to maintain this relationship. Instead of humans adapting to tools, we need tools that adapt to our humanity.

Project

The Mirrorwood Fables

I trained an AI on fables from around the world—Jataka, Panchatantra, Aesop, Aboriginal, Buddhist—plus my own writings. Now we use biofeedback to measure your psychosomatic landscape, feed it to the AI, and give you a story that shows your unconscious what's happening in your body. A chance to connect with things you didn't know you were feeling.

Part Three

Islands of Coherence

When a glass of water freezes, it doesn't freeze gradually. Little pockets freeze first. When enough pockets have frozen, the entire system shifts simultaneously. The context changes, and the whole field transforms.

The monks do not look at this time as disempowerment. They look at it as opportunity. What we choose, what we say, what islands of coherence we decide to play within—these shift the whole field. We are in a time where small choices echo.

If I walk with my values, it doesn't matter where I go. It's going to be valuable. The process matters.

North Star

To build cultural rituals that repair our relationship with ourselves, with others, with Mother Earth, and with our tools.
