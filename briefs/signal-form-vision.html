A studio in the distance

Scroll

In September 2026, there is a studio. It is small—four, maybe five people—but it is not small in what it does.

The studio exists because a government client said something unexpected. She said: "What do you need to make stuff happen?" And then she said: "I'll support whatever you propose."

That was the moment. Not a pitch. Not a proposal. A door, opening.

The Premise

Large institutions hire large consultancies. The consultancies staff projects with strategists—people who make PowerPoints and one-pagers and decks that say what should happen without ever making it happen.

But sometimes the client doesn't want more strategy. They want things. Interactive things. Experiences. Products that work differently than a PDF or a 30-second video.

The studio makes those things.

It is not an agency. It is not a consultancy. It is a production house with strategic capability—or a strategy practice with production muscle. The distinction matters less than the outcome: things get made.

The People

The studio is built from people who have worked together before. They know how each other think. They have shorthand. They've shipped work under pressure and covered for each other when things went sideways.

Some of them came from VCS, a creative team inside a large corporation that did good work until the corporation decided good work was not the priority. The studio is what VCS might have been if no one was watching.

The Structure

The studio is not trying to become a large consultancy. It does not want hundreds of employees or offices in multiple cities. It wants to remain small enough that everyone knows everyone, and large enough to take on meaningful work.

Client at a Time

The model is simple: one major client, deep focus, eight-week sprints. When the sprint ends, there's a thing. Not a deck about a thing. The thing itself.

"This is how this stuff happens. People that have worked together for a long time, know each other, and then everyone gets a little more senior, a little more experienced, and someone takes a chance and does a thing and gets lucky." — Greg, January 2026

The Demo

One government conference. One QR code. One interactive experience that shows what the studio can do. Doors open.

The Transition

Contract vehicle expires. The client says: we'd like to continue, directly. No intermediaries. The studio becomes the studio.

2027

The Expansion

A second client. Then a third. The model holds. The team grows, carefully. The work stays good.

The Question

Greg has been here before. He watched a three-person shop grow to ten in four years. He knows the paperwork, the pitfalls, the moments when you have to push back and the moments when you have to just figure it out.

The question Mike asked was simple:

"What would it take to do this together?"

And Greg's answer, after a long pause:

"This interests me so much more than I expected. This could be the career transition I've been wanting to do for five years—to actually build a business in a field where I have some confidence." — Greg

That's not a yes. But it's not a no.

The Horizon

The studio doesn't exist yet. But it could. The client gave the keys. The team is available. The timing is strange and perfect. All that remains is to walk toward it.
