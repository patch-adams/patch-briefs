The Premise

We lie to ourselves.
Not maliciously. Structurally.

We do it about our marriages, our politics, our health, our kids, our identities. The smarter you are, the better you are at it — because intelligence gives you the tools to contextualize anything.

This book is about what happens when you stop.

Chapter 01

Medicine

Jason is a bariatric surgeon. Every day he operates on patients whose bodies have been shaped by an epidemic that didn't exist within his parents' lifetime.

"We never had to make pills and shots for obesity. We never had to cut people's stomachs out. This only happened in the last 50 years. So why?"

This chapter isn't about blaming patients or the food industry. It's about the collective self-deception that allows a society to normalize a crisis — and the personal self-deception that keeps a surgeon from asking the harder questions about his own role in the system.

The authority conversation: a physician who's spent decades watching the medical establishment treat symptoms while ignoring causes — and who's ready to say so on the record.

Chapter 02

Marriage

Jason will tell you he has a great marriage. He'll also tell you marriage is tough. Most people can only say one of those things at a time.

The lie isn't that marriage is easy. The lie is that admitting it's hard means something is wrong. The performance of effortless partnership — on social media, at dinner parties, in front of the kids — is its own form of self-deception.

This chapter explores what happens when two people agree to stop performing. What honesty costs. What it builds. And why most couples would rather maintain the lie.

Chapter 03

Politics

Jason is a gun owner. He believes in the Second Amendment. And he'll be the first to tell you when his own side is lying to itself.

"If this shooting had happened under a Democratic president, 2A advocates would be mobilized. But silence under this administration? The NRA gave one sentence. The cognitive dissonance in our culture is fucking insane."

This isn't a chapter about gun control. It's about the tribal loyalties that override honest thinking — on both sides. It's about what it costs to call bullshit on your own people. And why almost nobody does.

The authority conversation: someone who's spent their career in the machinery of political communication — and who can articulate exactly how the self-deception gets manufactured at scale.

Chapter 04

Parenting

Jason has kids. He loves them completely. And he knows — really knows, in the way most parents won't say out loud — that they can fail. That good parenting doesn't come with a guarantee.

"My kids can fuck up any day, every day." That's not pessimism. That's the honest starting point that most parenting advice refuses to acknowledge.

This chapter is about the self-deception that parenting culture runs on: the belief that if you do everything right, the outcome is assured. The terror of admitting your children are autonomous beings who will make their own choices — including bad ones.

And the freedom that comes from accepting that. The permission to parent from honesty instead of fear.

Chapter 05

Identity

The deepest lies are the ones you don't know are lies. The stories you built your entire self-concept on — the ones that feel like bedrock but are actually scaffolding.

Jason wanted to write this book for years. What stopped him? The belief that he wasn't qualified. A bariatric surgeon who sees self-deception every day in his patients — was deceiving himself about his own right to say something about it.

This chapter goes to the foundation. The lies about who we are, what we deserve, what we're capable of. The identities we constructed in childhood that we're still living inside as adults. And what happens when you start pulling at those threads.

Chapter 06

Culture

Individual self-deception is one thing. But what about the lies a whole society agrees to believe?

Who told us we had to lie to ourselves? At what point did collective self-deception become not just tolerated but required — the price of admission to civil society?

This final chapter zooms out from the personal to the systemic. The cultural agreements to not look too closely. The shared fictions that hold institutions together. And the question of whether a society can function without them — or whether honesty at scale is its own kind of disruption.

The authority conversation: someone who studies how cultures construct and maintain their blind spots — and what happens when those constructions crack.
